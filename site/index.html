What I bring to a simulation team

The work maps onto the core of a robotics-sim role, and adds the one angle most simulation effort leaves out.

Model building

URDF and MJCF models with correct kinematics, dynamics, and mass properties taken from geometry.

Sim-to-real tuning

Physics parameters fit to data: friction, damping, inertia, and actuator behavior, then validated.

Actuator modeling

First-principles thermal and torque models that go beyond the constant-gain assumption.

Simulation pipelines

Excitation, logging, identification, and out-of-sample validation, wired end to end.

ROS2 integration

rclpy nodes that run a sim as a joint-state server under live torque commands.

Dynamics and control

Rigid-body dynamics, contact, gravity compensation, and model-based control.

RL environments

Gymnasium environments with defensible observation, action, and reward design, scripted baselines, and honest evaluation.

Verification and task design

Nonlinear structural analysis in OpenSees, and machine-gradable tasks with golden solutions checked on independent paths.

About

I am a PhD candidate in Mechanical and Industrial Engineering at the Rochester Institute of Technology, working on high-heat-flux thermal systems and physics-based modeling. That work is peer reviewed: publications in the ASME Journal of Heat and Mass Transfer and at IEEE ITherm, including thermal resistance network analysis of a two-phase cooling module, the same class of model that drives Project 2.

My research runs on the same foundations robotics simulation does: rigid-body mechanics, careful mass properties, numerical integration, and closing the distance between a model and a measurement. These projects apply that toolkit to MuJoCo, and lead with what heat does to an actuator under load.

These are portfolio demonstrations, documented to be read and reproduced, with all source and methods on GitHub. Every number on this site regenerates from the code. More projects are on the way.
